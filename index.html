Three things this game isn’t. And three it is.

✕ Not therapy

The Vault Rule means you can pass any card without explaining yourself. Boundary-setting is built in.

✕ Not trivia

Nobody is right. Nobody is being tested. The deck is on your side.

✕ Not a generic conversation deck

Built for people who have spent years translating themselves at the dinner table.

✓ A phone-down ritual

Phones go in the pouch before the first card. The game cannot start until they do.

✓ Cultural recognition with teeth

The kind of laugh that comes from being seen — and from finally putting words on a thing you’d stopped trying to explain.

✓ A box that gets heavier

The deck grows with you. House Cards are forever. Most card games end. This one keeps writing itself.

The friction is the feature

Phones in the pouch.
Hands on the cards.

Every box ships with two cloth pouches. Phones go in. Pouches zip closed. The game cannot start until they are. Designed to replace ninety minutes of doomscrolling, not ninety minutes of therapy.

You have seven days a week for your phone. Your relationship gets one hour.

The Vault Rule

Pass any card. No explanation needed. The Vault Rule is printed on every card and on the box.

Who it’s for

You just have to recognize at least one of these.

You don’t need a label or a perfect description. If any of these feels familiar, your voice belongs in this.

S

The “Subtitles” moment

When the room is laughing and you’re smiling along — understanding the words but not the joke.

?

The “Which one are you?” moment

When your child, or you, gets asked to check one box. As if identity worked like a multiple choice.

U

The Untranslatable

The word in your language that carries an entire philosophy. The concept your partner half-understands.

F

The Fading Tradition

The ritual your grandmother did, your mother did less, you almost never. The one you’re quietly afraid your kid won’t know.

R

The “Whose Rules?” moment

When a parenting disagreement isn’t really about parenting. It’s about two inherited ideas of what respect even means.

H

The “Where is Home?” moment

When someone asks and you pause. Not because you don’t know — because the honest answer is too long for small talk.

How this game found its founders
Bill & Lili Omondi · Nairobi → U.S.

We are Bill and Lili. We loved game nights; the laughter, the chaos, the shared references that made everyone feel included. But sometimes a single joke, a movie quote, or a pop culture reference could remind you what it feels like to be the only person in the room outside the story. Not because I didn’t understand the words, but because Icouldn’t access the invisible layer underneath.

So I did what a lot of people do when they don’t want to look stupid in a room full of people who all seem to belong: I declined to play.

So Bill did something else. He learned my language. He studied Kenya’s political history, the foods, the textures, the inside jokes of an entire civilization he hadn’t grown up inside. Not because he had to. Because he wanted to live inside my reality the way I had always been expected to live inside his.

He learned my language. That’s what brought us closer than any game ever had.
